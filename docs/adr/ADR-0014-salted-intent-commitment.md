# ADR-0014 — Salted / hiding v:1 intent commitment (closes RCPT‑001)

**Status: ACCEPTED — implemented & tested (`receipts/src/receipt.ts`, `disclosure/src/selective.ts`,
`planes/src/node.ts`, conformance C23). Construction is a salted SHA3 commitment in the classical ROM;
UNAUDITED like the rest of the disclosure layer — no formal soundness/hiding claim until external audit.**
Date: 2026‑06‑21. Direct response to **RCPT‑001** from the Team Apex enforcement‑boundary audit
([council/team-apex-zkrange-2026-06-21.md](../council/team-apex-zkrange-2026-06-21.md)).

## Context

A v:1 receipt commits to the decision's inputs by hash. The intent commitment was:

```
commitments.intent = SHA3‑256(encodeCanonical(intent))
```

and the **receipt body IS the public transparency‑log leaf** (`receiptLeaf = encodeCanonical(body)`), so
`commitments.intent` is published to every log observer.

`ActionIntent` carries a low‑entropy `amount` (and other small, guessable fields). **RCPT‑001:** a log
observer who knows the rest of the intent can **brute‑force the amount** — enumerate candidate amounts,
recompute `SHA3‑256(encodeCanonical(intent‑with‑candidate))`, and match the published hash. Amount domains
are small, so this is trivial. SHA3 preimage‑resistance does **not** help: the preimage is low‑entropy and
its structure is known (this is exactly the false‑clear error Hermes made in the audit).

This is the **same brute‑force class as CB‑001** (already fixed in `disclosure/commitbind.ts` by *omitting*
the amount from the public binding digest) — but here it lives in the v:1 receipt that is **actually
logged**, and the amount cannot simply be omitted: the receipt must commit to the *whole* intent so an
authorized verifier can later disclose it selectively against the log.

The project already names this exact hazard ("unsalted selective‑disclosure brute‑force over small
enumerable domains") in `policyproof.ts` and `selective.ts`; the v:1 intent hash re‑introduced it.

## Decision

Make the intent commitment **hiding** by salting it with a **high‑entropy, per‑receipt salt**:

```
intentSalt          = CSPRNG(32 bytes)              # 256‑bit, fresh per receipt
commitments.intent  = SHA3‑256(encodeCanonical({ d: "Nerion/disclosure/salted-commit/v1",
                                                  salt: intentSalt,
                                                  value: intent }))
```

- The salt is generated by the issuer (`buildReceipt`, with the orchestrating `PolarSeekNode` minting it
  explicitly per admission) and carried on the **`Receipt` wrapper** (`receipt.intentSalt`), alongside the
  signature and issuer key.
- The salt is **NEVER part of `ReceiptBody`**, therefore never part of `receiptLeaf` / the signed body / the
  published log leaf. A log observer sees only the salted hash.
- The salt is revealed **only to authorized verifiers**, out of band, for **selective disclosure**:
  `selective.ts` `commitField(value, salt?)` / `verifyDisclosure(hash, value, salt?)` now accept the salt,
  and `receipts/receipt.ts` exposes `verifyIntentDisclosure(receipt, revealedIntent)` (a thin wrapper that
  recomputes the salted commitment from `receipt.intentSalt`).
- dCBOR with a domain tag makes the preimage unambiguous (sorted keys, definite lengths, byte‑string salt —
  no concatenation‑splitting), and domain‑separates this commitment from other SHA3 uses.

The salt is optional in `commitField`/`verifyDisclosure` so the **unsalted** form (`SHA3(canonical(value))`)
remains available for any existing caller. (The receipt's `capability` and `policy` commitments were
originally left unsalted here but are now ALSO salted — see decision #4 / RCPT‑PRIV‑001 — so the whole
published leaf is unlinkable, not just the `intent`.)

### Why salt‑and‑hash here, not Pedersen

The intent commitment must stay a **plain, externally‑recomputable SHA3 hash** anchored in the RFC 6962 log
and reusable by the dCBOR / transparency tooling and the existing selective‑disclosure machinery. A salted
hash is the **minimal, audit‑trivial** fix that turns a binding‑only commitment into a binding **and** hiding
one (ROM hiding under a 256‑bit salt). A Pedersen commitment is algebraic and exists to support **ZK proofs
about a hidden amount** — that is a different job (see relationship below), not the right tool for hiding the
whole‑intent log hash.

## Relationship to the v:2 PSP / Pedersen path (ADR‑0006, ADR‑0013)

Amount **privacy is ultimately v:2's job** — this ADR does **not** change that, and the two layers are
complementary, not substitutes:

| | v:1 salted intent commitment (this ADR) | v:2 PSP / Pedersen (ADR‑0006 / ADR‑0013) |
|---|---|---|
| Object | `SHA3` over the **whole intent** | Pedersen `C = a·G + r·H` over the **amount** only |
| Property added | **Hiding** of the log leaf (was binding‑only) | **ZK proofs** *about* the hidden amount (range / policy‑satisfaction) + commitment‑to‑intent binding |
| Disclosure model | Reveal the intent **in full** to an authorized verifier (selective disclosure) | Reveal **nothing** — prove a predicate (e.g. `amount ≤ ceiling`) |
| Hiding strength | Classical ROM, 256‑bit salt | Information‑theoretic / PQ for the commitment itself |

So:

- This ADR closes the **immediate, deployment‑relevant log leak** (RCPT‑001) for the v:1 receipt that is
  actually logged today, with a small, low‑risk schema change.
- It does **not** deliver amount‑privacy‑*with‑proofs*. A verifier authorized to see the intent still sees
  the amount on disclosure — that is selective disclosure working as intended. Proving properties of an
  amount *without revealing it* remains the **v:2 PSP/Pedersen** path.
- The layers **compose**: a future v:2 receipt keeps the salted (hiding) intent commitment for the log leaf
  **and** carries the Pedersen `C` (structurally bound per ADR‑0013) for ZK amount proofs. The salt does not
  obviate v:2; v:2 does not obviate the salt.

## Alternatives considered

1. **Omit the amount from the intent commitment (as CB‑001 did) — REJECTED for v:1.** The receipt must
   commit to the complete intent so an authorized party can disclose/recompute it from the log; dropping the
   amount would make the logged commitment unable to attest the actual decided amount and would split the
   commitment scheme. CB‑001 could omit it because the amount there is bound *cryptographically* by a
   separate Pedersen commitment; v:1 has no such commitment.
2. **Per‑field salts (one salt per intent field) — DEFERRED.** A single per‑receipt salt over the whole
   intent already makes every field (including `amount`) non‑brute‑forceable from the leaf and keeps
   disclosure simple (reveal intent + one salt). Per‑field salts would enable *field‑granular* disclosure
   without revealing siblings; that is a selective‑disclosure ergonomics upgrade, orthogonal to RCPT‑001, and
   can layer on later.
3. **Encrypt the intent to authorized verifiers instead of committing — REJECTED.** Key management +
   confidentiality is a heavier, separate trust model; the log only needs a *hiding commitment*, which a
   salted hash provides without any new key material.
4. **Salt the other commitments (`capability`, `policy`) too — DONE (RCPT‑PRIV‑001, 2026‑07‑03).**
   Originally deferred, but the cross-cutting privacy-composition review showed an unsalted
   `commitments.capability` is a STABLE deterministic fingerprint (a `Capability` is a fully
   deterministic grant chain), so a passive log observer could cluster the whole log by it — re-linking
   a holder's actions and defeating the very unlinkability this ADR adds to `intent`, one field over.
   Both are now salted with the same off-leaf `intentSalt` via `commitField(value, salt)`, so the WHOLE
   leaf is unlinkable to a passive observer while an authorized party holding the salt can still
   recompute the commitment.

## Consequences

- **Schema:** `Receipt` gains `intentSalt: Bytes` (NOT in `ReceiptBody`/leaf). `BuildReceiptParams` gains an
  **optional** `intentSalt` (override for determinism/testing; defaults to a fresh 32‑byte CSPRNG salt).
  `PolarSeekNode.admit` mints the salt per admission and it rides out on `outcome.receipt.intentSalt`.
- **Verification paths unchanged in trust:** `verifyReceipt` / `verifyReceiptInclusion` still verify the
  signature + leaf↔body binding + Merkle inclusion using only the body — they neither need nor receive the
  salt, so external inclusion verification is unaffected. Selective intent disclosure is a *separate*,
  authorized step (`verifyIntentDisclosure` / `verifyDisclosure(..., salt)`).
- **Unlinkability:** a fresh salt per receipt means two receipts for the *same* intent produce *different*
  intent commitments — removing a cross‑receipt correlation channel on the amount. (Conversely, the salt
  **must** be unique per receipt; reusing one would relink and weaken hiding — enforced by always generating
  fresh CSPRNG bytes.)
- **Conformance:** new check **C23** asserts the leaf does not contain the salt, a salt‑less observer cannot
  recompute the commitment, and an authorized verifier (with the salt) can disclose it while a wrong salt /
  tampered amount is rejected.
- **Docs:** [ASSURANCE.md](../ASSURANCE.md) "Amount confidentiality" row updated from *overclaim‑corrected
  leak* to *fixed (salted) for the log leaf; full amount‑privacy‑with‑proofs still v:2*.

## Trust model / limits (honest)

- **Hiding is computational (ROM) and rests on the salt staying secret to unauthorized parties** and on
  256‑bit salt entropy — not information‑theoretic. It is **not** the PQ/info‑theoretic hiding of the
  Pedersen commitment.
- **No defense against a malicious issuer is added or needed here** — the issuer authors the intent and the
  salt and gains nothing by manipulating its own salt. Issuer‑honesty at admission remains the
  quorum/attestation model's job (unchanged).
- **Disclosure reveals the full intent** to whoever is authorized — by design. Predicate‑only privacy is v:2.
- **UNAUDITED.** Like the rest of `disclosure/`, this is an internal‑review‑level construction; the external
  ROS / ToB audit still applies. This ADR records a *design + implementation decision*, not a security
  result.
